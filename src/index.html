Discover the Paris Museums

When you go in a museum, you look at the art work but do you realize the museum itself is a work of art ? Welcome to the virtual Museum of Museums ! Start to discover the main Museums of Paris and learn about their creation, architecture and story.

Musée du Louvre

The first Louvre castle was built in 1202. The construction of the Louvre spans more than 800 years. Some parts have been destroyed and others added over the years. As a result, the palace has different architectural styles. However, most of the structures that make the Musée du Louvre today are in the style of the French Renaissance. The features of this period included the greater use of the ancient Classical orders of columns and pilasters and a wide variety of decorative plaques and sculptural decoration on the facades.

Musée d'Orsay

The Musée d’Orsay was built from 1898 to 1900. It was designed by the french architect Victor Latour. This building belongs to the Beaux-Arts architectural style. This style was taught at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris, particularly from the 1830s to the end of the 19th century. It drew upon the principles of French neoclassicism, but also incorporated Gothic and Renaissance elements, and used modern materials, such as iron and glass.

Centre Pompidou

The Centre Pompidou was built from 1971 to 1977. It was designed by the italian architects Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers. This building has become a true emblem of contemporary art. The idea of Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers was to to exteriorize the inside of the building. Usually hidden, the entire load-bearing structure as well as the piping is discharged outside the building. The building is upside down ! This frees up interior space for activities and makes the layout more flexible and modifiable.

Grand Palais

The Grand Palais was built from 1897 to 1900. It was designed by the french architects Henri Deglane, Albert Louvet, Albert-Félix-Théophile Thomas and Charles Girault. This building belongs to the Beaux-Arts architectural style. This style was taught at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris, particularly from the 1830s to the end of the 19th century.
